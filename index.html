My role

I led Task 4: CoLiving, owning the end-to-end UX process from wireframing and interaction design to usability testing and content strategy. The feature covers expense splitting, chore tracking, and shared living dashboards. Beyond my own task, I led the team usability study and content-fidelity analysis — synthesizing findings across all four flows to ensure our research translated into real design decisions.

Wireframes

The CoLiving feature required balancing a lot of data — expenses, balances, chore assignments, due dates — without overwhelming users. Early wireframes explored how to organize this into a dashboard that felt organized rather than overwhelming.

Challenges

🔗

Design consistency across 4 tasks

Coordinating layout, terminology, and navigation patterns across four independently developed flows required constant alignment to make everything feel like one unified app.

📊

Information overload in CoLiving

Users in our study flagged screen fatigue from too much content upfront. That pushed me to rethink visual hierarchy and prioritize what mattered most on first glance.

↩

Back navigation as a pain point

Across multiple tasks, unclear back navigation undermined user confidence. It reinforced that even small decisions — like a clearly labeled back button — can make or break a user's experience.

The solution

A fully integrated prototype that guides users from apartment discovery all the way through move-in management, search, 3D virtual tour, maintenance requests, and roommate coordination, unified in one cohesive system. The CoLiving feature specifically reduced cognitive load by surfacing the most relevant information first and using status cards, progress bars, and filters to give users a clear mental model of their shared living situation at a glance.

Impact

  • Delivered a fully interactive prototype spanning 4 task flows end-to-end
  • Usability study with 4 participants scored Excellent
  • 100% task completion rate across all 4 participants
  • Designed with mobile-first patterns: tabs, filters, progress bars, status cards
  • Coordinated design consistency across a 4-person cross-functional team

Reflection

This project reinforced that great UX isn't just about how something looks — it's about how clearly it thinks for the user. Designing for the full journey, not just the entry point, means anticipating every state a user might be in and making sure the product meets them there.

My role

I led Task 4: More Like This — a personalized style recommendation feature. I owned the end-to-end UX process from hand-sketched wireframes and Figma prototyping to AI image generation, interaction design, and documentation. Beyond my task, I recruited and conducted a usability study session, wrote the Content Fidelity Matrix, and contributed to the team peer critique.

The feature

More Like This lets users select a clothing item from their personal closet using color-coded category overlays, then browse AI-curated similar items with price, color, and style filters. Users can compare options side-by-side with Before/After previews and save directly to their Wishlist or send to their Personal Fitting Room.

Screen 1 — Personal Closet & Item Selection

Screen 2 — Recommendations Dashboard with Filters

Screen 3 — Item Details & Before/After Comparison

Screen 4 — Wishlist & Cart Confirmation

Challenges

🎨

Design consistency across 4 tasks

Keeping four individually built tasks visually and structurally consistent required constant alignment on layout, navigation patterns, and terminology to make everything feel like one app.

📋

Information overload in recommendations

The recommendation screen struggled with too much content early on. Usability feedback pushed me to rethink visual hierarchy and cut what wasn't earning its space on screen.

🖱

Button placement and contrast

Two participants misclicked between "Add to Cart" and "Save to Wishlist" because the buttons lacked visual contrast. Small UI decisions had a bigger impact than expected.

How AI was used

Throughout Task 4 I used Figma AI to generate realistic fashion imagery for the prototype screens. Each prompt was carefully crafted to produce headless, torso-focused editorial shots that maintained a consistent neutral aesthetic across the recommendation cards — keeping the focus on the clothing rather than the model.

Impact

  • Fully interactive desktop prototype spanning 4 task flows end-to-end
  • Usability study with real participants rated Good to Excellent on the SUS scale
  • 100% task completion rate across all 4 participants
  • Used Figma AI to generate fashion imagery with detailed, intentional prompts
  • Coordinated design consistency across a 4-person cross-functional team

Reflection

This project reinforced that great UX is about how clearly a product thinks for the user — not just how it looks. Designing a recommendation feature means anticipating what users are looking for before they can fully articulate it, and making sure every filter, every card, and every confirmation state earns its place.
