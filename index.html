My role

I led Task 4: CoLiving, owning the end-to-end UX process from wireframing and interaction design to usability testing and content strategy. The feature covers expense splitting, chore tracking, and shared living dashboards. Beyond my own task, I led the team usability study and content-fidelity analysis — synthesizing findings across all four flows to ensure our research translated into real design decisions.

Wireframes

The CoLiving feature required balancing a lot of data — expenses, balances, chore assignments, due dates — without overwhelming users. Early wireframes explored how to organize this into a dashboard that felt organized rather than overwhelming.

Challenges

🔗

Design consistency across 4 tasks

Coordinating layout, terminology, and navigation patterns across four independently developed flows required constant alignment to make everything feel like one unified app.

📊

Information overload in CoLiving

Users in our study flagged screen fatigue from too much content upfront. That pushed me to rethink visual hierarchy and prioritize what mattered most on first glance.

↩

Back navigation as a pain point

Across multiple tasks, unclear back navigation undermined user confidence. It reinforced that even small decisions — like a clearly labeled back button — can make or break a user's experience.

The solution

A fully integrated prototype that guides users from apartment discovery all the way through move-in management, search, 3D virtual tour, maintenance requests, and roommate coordination, unified in one cohesive system. The CoLiving feature specifically reduced cognitive load by surfacing the most relevant information first and using status cards, progress bars, and filters to give users a clear mental model of their shared living situation at a glance.

Impact

  • Delivered a fully interactive prototype spanning 4 task flows end-to-end
  • Usability study with 4 participants scored Excellent
  • 100% task completion rate across all 4 participants
  • Designed with mobile-first patterns: tabs, filters, progress bars, status cards
  • Coordinated design consistency across a 4-person cross-functional team

Reflection

This project reinforced that great UX isn't just about how something looks,it's about how clearly it thinks for the user. Designing for the full journey, not just the entry point, means anticipating every state a user might be in and making sure the product meets them there.

None of this would have come together without my team. Every one of them brought something essential to this project.
